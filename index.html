Self-Regulation and Self-Discipline Framework with Cognitive Biases

Purpose: Identify how cognitive biases disrupt the development of key reflective and executive capacities.

SELF-REGULATION

Sustains goal-directed behavior by (a) inhibiting impulsive responses, (b) directing and sustaining attention, and (c) regulating emotion under pressure, distraction, or uncertainty.

1. Sense-Making

Constructs a coherent working model of reality by (a) integrating metacognitive signals, (b) incorporating allocentric perspectives, (c) reconciling conflicts across inputs, and (d) revising priors when warranted.

1a. Critical Thinking

Evaluates assertions by (a) estimating plausibility, (b) assessing source reliability, and (c) stress-testing for logic and evidence.

  • Confirmation Bias – Doesn’t try to disprove
  • Base Rate Neglect – Overlooks general statistical trends
  • Myside Bias – Hesitates to consider differing perspectives
  • Belief Bias – Mixes up what sounds true with what’s logically sound
  • Certainty Effect – Overlooks uncertainty
  • Probability Neglect – Disregards odds when emotions run high

1b. Objectivity

Identifies uneven standards, then applies (a) empirical and (b) rational criteria evenly across all beliefs.

Example: Applies the “veil of ignorance.”

  • Double Standard Bias – Uses different rules for different cases
  • Selective Perception – Sees things in a way that fits personal views
  • Status Quo Bias – Clings to existing beliefs
  • Projection Bias – Assumes others think the same way

1c. Fairness

Applies balance between (a) empathy and (b) accountability norms, regardless of status or convenience.

Example: Admits mistakes to prevent double standards and hypocrisy.

  • Egocentric Bias – Reduces ability to empathize with others
  • Fundamental Attribution Error – Blocks holding others accountable fairly
  • Actor-Observer Bias – Excuses self, blames others
  • Just-World Hypothesis – Judges morally instead of contextually

1d. Self-Reflection

Analyzes outcomes to (a) detect patterns, (b) identify levers, and (c) convert insight into action without rumination.

Format: “If X cue occurs, then I will do Y behavior within Z time.”

  • Self-Serving Bias – Prevents learning from failure
  • Outcome Bias – Judges based on results, not the process
  • Hindsight Bias – Distorts how we revise our past views

1e. Self-Efficacy

Maintains confidence by (a) anchoring belief in evidence, (b) interpreting setbacks constructively, and (c) testing skills regularly.

  • Overconfidence – Overrates personal ability
  • Dunning-Kruger Effect – Gets skill level wrong
  • Impostor Syndrome – Doubts real accomplishments
  • Negativity Bias – Focuses too much on failures

SELF-DISCIPLINE

Applies self-regulation to align behavior with long-term goals and principles by (a) selecting priorities, (b) sustaining execution, and (c) avoiding rigidity or complacency.

2a. Perseverance

Sustains effort amid hardship by (a) countering avoidance impulses, (b) reaffirming commitment, and (c) reducing fear-driven withdrawal through exposure and skill-building.

Examples: Treats fear as trainable, uses graduated exposure to build resilience.

  • Loss Aversion – Panics about failing
  • Threat Bias – Avoids hard things out of fear
  • Present Bias – Ignores long-term benefits

2b. Temperance

Allocates willpower by (a) ranking demands, (b) reducing decision load, and (c) using "good-enough" regulation to focus on what matters.

Example: Compresses emotions into a single label to regain focus.

  • Decision Fatigue – Makes self-control fade over time
  • Choice Overload – Tires out the mind with too many options
  • Sunk Cost Fallacy – Stays stuck due to what’s already invested
  • Planning Fallacy – Thinks tasks will take less time and effort than they do
