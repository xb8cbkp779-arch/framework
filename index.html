Self-Regulation:

Manages impulses, attention, and emotions via a cycle of self-monitoring, analysis and self-adjusting.

Sense-Making:

Converts raw data into structured schemas by integrating sensory (perceptions and internal states) and symbolic (language and concepts) signals, while reconciling their discrepancies, addressing allocentric perspectives, and revising preconceptions.

Critical Thinking:

Evaluates propositions by estimating their plausibility and reliability, while assessing whether arguments are logically valid and evidence is relevant to the propositions. This process clarifies the relationships among claims, evidence, and inference, leading to cohesive, well-supported conclusions.

Objectivity:

Mitigates motivated reasoning by identifying uneven evidentiary standards, then uniformly applying empirical and rational criteria to all conclusions through self-critique.

Fairness:

Applies an impartial balance between empathy modulated by inferred intent and accountability modulated by consequence, in reciprocal interactions, regardless of status or perceived moral standing.

Self-Reflection:

Causal attribution that involves separating personal choices from external circumstances, identifying recurring patterns and controllable levers of influence, and converting insights into action.

Self-Efficacy:

Grounds self-belief in past achievements, frames setbacks as temporary skill gaps rather than personal flaws, and periodically reassesses skills to pit subjective belief against objective competence.

Self-Discipline:

The establishment of priorities and the persistent implementation of goals, achieved by voluntarily inhibiting “wants” to pursue “shoulds” amid internal conflict, thus aligning behavior to long-term objectives.

Temperance:

Allocates effort according to strategic importance rather than sunk costs, minimizes unnecessary decision load or temptation exposure, and selects regulation strategies that preserve resources for highest priorities.

Perseverance:

Goal-directed persistence of action, through the overriding of avoidance urges, committing to valued outcomes, and utilizing gradual exposure and skill-development to reduce fear-driven withdrawal.
