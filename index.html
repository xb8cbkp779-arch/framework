Self-Regulation

0. SELF-REGULATION

The Modulation of impulses, attention, and emotions in the presence of pressure, distraction, or uncertainty.

1. Sense-Making

(a) integrates metacognitive signals (confidence, bias cues), incorporates allocentric perspectives and base rates, reconciles discrepancies across inputs, and revises preconceptions when evidence demands it; constructing a coherent model of reality.

1a. Critical Thinking

Evaluates assertions by estimating the prior plausibility of the proposition, (b) assessing source reliability, (c) distingushing between claim, evidence, and inference as separate justificatory layers, and (d) evaluating logical validity and evidential relevance.

Behavioral markers: (a) distinguishes claim from evidence, (b) seeks base rates, (c) attempts falsification rather than confirmation, and (d) tracks uncertainty explicitly.

1b. Objectivity

Mitigates motivated reasoning by identifying uneven evidentiary standards, then uniformly applying: empirical criteria (measurement and replication), and rational criteria (non-contradiction, Bayes-updating), to both favored and disfavored conclusions.

1c. Fairness

Maintains impartial balance in reciprocal interactions by weighing: empathy regarding context and intent, against: accountability norms regarding reciprocity and consequence; irrespective of status, convenience, mood, or perceived moral superiority.

Example: Admits own mistakes to prevent double standards and hypocrisy.

1d. Self-Reflection

Accurate causal attribution by: distinguishing personal choices from external circumstances, identifying recurring patterns and controllable levers of influence, and the conversion of insight into actionable rules.

1e. Self-Efficacy

Grounds self-belief firmly in verified past achievements, frames setbacks as temporary skill gaps rather than personal flaws, and periodically reassesses skills to keep them sharp and confirm current capabilities.

Examples: (a) integrates specific achievements into self-concept, and (b) utilizes setbacks as training data.

2. Self-Discipline

The establishment of priorities, the persistent implementation of goals , and the evasion of rigidity and complacency; thus aligning behavior with long-term objectives.

2a. Temperance

Manages willpower as a finite resource by: allocating effort according to current strategic importance rather than sunk costs, minimizing unnecessary decision load and temptation exposure, and selecting regulation strategies that preserve capacity for highest-priority objectives.

Example: Compresses emotions into a single label to reducing processing load and restore focus.

2b. Perseverance

Sustains effort amid adversity by: overriding avoidance impulses, reaffirming commitment to valued outcomes, and utilizing deliberate exposure and skill-development to reduce fear-driven withdrawal over time.

Examples: (a) Interprets fear as a manageable physiological signal, and (b) employs graduated exposure to build lasting resilience.
