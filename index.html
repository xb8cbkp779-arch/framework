Self-Regulation and Cognitive Bias Framework

Purpose: To map biases to self-regulatory capacities using a structured cognitive framework.

1. SELF-REGULATION

Definition: Sustains goal-directed behavior by (a) inhibiting impulsive responses, (b) directing and sustaining attention, and (c) regulating emotion under pressure, distraction, or uncertainty.

1. Sense-Making

Constructs a coherent working model of reality by (a) integrating metacognitive signals (confidence, bias cues, emotional arousal), (b) incorporating allocentric perspectives (outside-view, other agents’ models, base rates), (c) reconciling conflicts across inputs, and (d) revising priors when warranted.

1a. Critical Thinking

Evaluates an assertion by (a) estimating the plausibility of its proposition, (b) assessing source reliability, and (c) stress-testing justification for logical validity and evidential relevance.

Behavioral markers: (a) distinguishes claim vs. evidence, (b) seeks base rates, (c) attempts falsification, (d) tracks uncertainty explicitly.

  • Confirmation Bias – Doesn’t try to disprove (instead of doing so)
  • Base Rate Neglect – Overlooks general statistical trends
  • Myside Bias – Hesitates to consider differing perspectives
  • Belief Bias – Mixes up what sounds true with what’s logically sound
  • Certainty Effect – Overlooks uncertainty
  • Probability Neglect – Disregards odds when emotions run high
1b. Objectivity

Identifies uneven standards in belief formation, then applies (a) empirical criteria (measurement, replication, base rates) and (b) rational criteria (coherence, non-contradiction, Bayes-consistent updating) uniformly across favored and disfavored conclusions.

Example: Applies the “veil of ignorance.”

  • Double Standard Bias – Uses different rules for different cases
  • Selective Perception – Sees things in a way that fits personal views
  • Status Quo Bias – Clings to existing beliefs
  • Projection Bias – Assumes others think the same way
1c. Fairness

Applies impartial balance between (a) empathy (context, constraints, intent) and (b) accountability norms (reciprocity, consequences, repair) in reciprocal interactions, irrespective of status, convenience, or mood.

Example: Admits mistakes to prevent double standards and avoid hypocrisy.

  • Egocentric Bias – Reduces ability to empathize with others
  • Fundamental Attribution Error – Blocks holding others accountable fairly
  • Actor-Observer Bias – Excuses self, blames others
  • Just-World Hypothesis – Judges morally instead of contextually
1d. Self-Reflection

Analyzes past outcomes to (a) detect recurring patterns, (b) identify controllable levers, and (c) convert insight into implementation rules, avoiding rumination by translating reflection into testable action.

Format: “If X cue occurs, then I will do Y behavior within Z time.”

  • Self-Serving Bias – Prevents learning from failure
  • Outcome Bias – Judges based on results, not the process
  • Hindsight Bias – Distorts how we revise our past views
1e. Self-Efficacy

Maintains confidence calibrated to evidence by (a) anchoring self-belief in demonstrated competencies, (b) interpreting setbacks as skill gaps or strategy mismatches rather than global self-worth deficits, and (c) preventing skill atrophy through periodic re-testing.

Examples: (a) integrates achievements into self-concept, (b) treats setbacks as training data.

  • Overconfidence – Overrates personal ability
  • Dunning-Kruger Effect – Gets skill level wrong
  • Impostor Syndrome – Doubts real accomplishments
  • Negativity Bias – Focuses too much on failures

2. SELF-DISCIPLINE

Definition: Applies self-regulation consistently to align behavior with long-term goals and principles by (a) selecting priorities, (b) sustaining execution, and (c) preventing drift into rigidity or complacency.

2a. Perseverance

Sustains effort amid hardship by (a) countering avoidance impulses, (b) reaffirming commitment to the target outcome, and (c) using exposure and skill-building to reduce fear-driven withdrawal over time.

Examples: (a) treats fear responses as trainable signals, (b) uses graduated exposure to build resilience.

  • Loss Aversion – Panics about failing
  • Threat Bias – Avoids hard things out of fear
  • Present Bias – Ignores long-term benefits
2b. Temperance

Allocates willpower as a limited resource by (a) ranking demands by importance, (b) reducing decision load and temptation bandwidth, and (c) choosing “good-enough” regulation strategies that preserve capacity for what matters most.

Example: Compresses emotions into a single label to regain focus.

  • Decision Fatigue – Makes self-control fade over time
  • Choice Overload – Tires out the mind with too many options
  • Sunk Cost Fallacy – Stays stuck due to what’s already invested
  • Planning Fallacy – Thinks tasks will take less time and effort than they do
